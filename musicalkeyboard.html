Musical keyboard

Co-design with people with Dementia

Our goal was to develop a technology that met the cognitive needs of people with mild Alzheimer's disease (AD) and enable them to participate in active music therapy (AMT) sessions without requiring assistance.

The plan

The process

Getting to know our users

I conducted initial interviews with healthcare professionals, dementia experts, and individuals with mild AD to identify the primary user group. To gain a deeper understanding of AD and create accurate personas, I researched scientific articles, books, videos, internet blogs, and online communities.

Exploring what’s already out there

I reviewed the scientific literature to gather recommended practices for autonomous AMT in people with AD and to identify what has been implemented to facilitate autonomous usage of electronic devices in this population. These insights based on scientific evidence guided the rest of the design process.

I conducted a scientific literature review to find best practices to enable individuals with AD to participate in AMT autonomously and use electronic devices independently. The scientific evidence obtained from the research influenced and guided the entire design process.

Ideation

I collaborated with the team to discuss the device's requirements, technological resources and limitations, and user experience in five meetings. To aid in brainstorming, I presented paper prototypes of varying sizes, shapes, and materials.

Electronic device

Our design was inspired by the intuitive relationship between the position of keys and the increasing frequency of their tone on a piano. Furthermore, research shows seniors find piano games really engaging.

We began by creating two key layouts for the device - "horizontal" and "inverted U" - to get user feedback and validate our design.

Therapeutic tasks

We created tasks that aimed to improve cognitive-motor planning and working memory. The tasks involved using a spaced retrieval technique which required users to listen, memorize, and play melodies.

Design, test, iterate

To learn more about users' preferences and abilities, I conducted individual co-design sessions with four participants. During these sessions, participants interacted with paper prototypes and chose from different materials and melodies to modify the appearance and experience of the device. They also tested the spaced retrieval tasks through a video game on a tablet.

Prototype testing

I created a prototype app to test the difficulty of each task and evaluate the clarity, relevance, and modality of the instructions (graphic, text, audio). Participants were guided through the song selection phase and then tested the tasks developed in the co-design session directly on the tablet.


”...challenging but fun”
Sessions with caregivers and researchers

To gather diverse perspectives and expertise on the device and its musical tasks, we held two focus group sessions with seven researchers from different fields (rehabilitation technology, dementia, neuroplasticity) and four caregivers of people with AD. These discussions validated and enhanced our previous research.
